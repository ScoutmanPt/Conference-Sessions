OXYGEN THERAPY ADMINISTRATION SOP
Version: 2.0 | Effective: 01 October 2024 | Review: 01 October 2026
Department: Respiratory / Nursing | Author: Dr. Brian Lowe, Respiratory Consultant

PURPOSE
To ensure safe and effective administration of supplemental oxygen therapy to adult inpatients at Northgate Regional Hospital, in accordance with BTS Guideline on Emergency Oxygen Use in Adult Patients.

KEY PRINCIPLE: Oxygen is a DRUG. It must be prescribed (except in emergencies). Oxygen should be titrated to achieve target saturation, not given at fixed high flow rates to all patients.

1. OXYGEN PRESCRIBING

Oxygen must be prescribed on the drug chart with:
- Target oxygen saturation range
- Delivery device
- Flow rate range

Standard target saturations:
  Most patients:                         94-98% SpO2
  COPD or at risk of hypercapnic failure: 88-92% SpO2

WARNING: Giving high-flow oxygen to patients with COPD and chronic hypercapnia can suppress their hypoxic drive, worsen hypercapnia, and cause respiratory failure. Always check for COPD diagnosis and use 88-92% target range.

In emergencies (cardiac arrest, major trauma, anaphylaxis, severe asthma, CO poisoning):
Give high-flow oxygen via non-rebreathe mask 15L/min without waiting for prescription.

2. DELIVERY DEVICES AND FLOW RATES

Nasal Cannulae:
  Flow: 1-4 L/min
  Approximate FiO2: 24-36%
  Use: Mild hypoxaemia; comfortable for long-term use; patient can eat and speak

Simple Face Mask:
  Flow: 5-10 L/min (NEVER below 5 L/min -- risk of CO2 rebreathing)
  Approximate FiO2: 35-50%
  Use: Moderate hypoxaemia

Venturi Mask (Controlled Oxygen Therapy):
  Available: 24%, 28%, 35%, 40%, 60%
  Use: COPD patients requiring precise FiO2 control
  MUST use correct colour-coded Venturi valve at correct flow rate (printed on valve)

Non-Rebreathe Mask (NRM):
  Flow: 15 L/min minimum
  Approximate FiO2: up to 90%
  Use: Emergency situations only -- not for routine use

3. MONITORING

Monitor SpO2 continuously in acutely unwell patients.
For stable patients, document SpO2 and flow rate:
- Every 4 hours on a clinical observation chart
- 30 minutes after any change in oxygen therapy
- If any change in clinical condition

If SpO2 falls below target:
  - Increase flow rate by 1-2 L/min (or move to higher-delivery device)
  - Reassess within 5-10 minutes
  - If no improvement: call medical team urgently
  - If SpO2 below 90% unresponsive to oxygen: call 2222

If SpO2 above target (hyperoxaemia):
  - Reduce flow rate or wean to room air if clinically appropriate
  - Document in patient record

4. WEANING OXYGEN

Wean oxygen gradually when patient is clinically stable and SpO2 consistently within target range on current therapy. Do not abruptly discontinue oxygen in acutely unwell patients.

Step-down approach:
NRM 15L -> Simple mask 10L -> Simple mask 5L -> Nasal cannulae 4L -> Nasal cannulae 2L -> Air

5. HUMIDIFICATION

Humidification is required when:
- Flow rate >4 L/min via nasal cannulae for more than 12 hours
- Patient has tracheostomy
- Patient reports excessive nasal dryness or bleeding

Use: Bubble humidifier or heated humidification circuit
