INSULIN ADMINISTRATION SAFETY SOP
Version: 5.0 | Effective: 01 January 2025 | Review: 01 January 2027
Department: Diabetes & Endocrinology / Nursing | Author: Ms. Claire Doyle, Diabetes Specialist Nurse

PURPOSE
Insulin is a HIGH-RISK medication. Administration errors can be immediately life-threatening.
This SOP must be followed for ALL insulin administration across the Trust.

INSULIN MUST NEVER be abbreviated as "U" for units on any prescription. Always write "units" in full.

1. THE FIVE RIGHTS OF INSULIN ADMINISTRATION

Two-Nurse Check: All insulin doses MUST be independently checked by two registered nurses before administration.

Right Patient:  Confirm identity using open questions + wristband check (Name, DOB, NHS number)
Right Drug:     Read the insulin name in full -- do NOT assume. Confirm type (rapid/short/intermediate/long-acting).
Right Dose:     Check dose against BG reading and insulin prescription chart.
Right Time:     Mealtime insulin given immediately before or immediately after food starts.
Right Route:    All insulin is subcutaneous (SC) UNLESS specifically prescribed IV in a clinical emergency.

2. SC INSULIN ADMINISTRATION -- STEP BY STEP

1. Check Blood Glucose (BG) before every insulin dose
2. Wash hands. Wear non-sterile gloves.
3. Verify insulin type and confirm correct insulin pen/vial
   - Cloudy insulin: Roll gently 10x, invert 10x to mix
   - Clear insulin: No mixing required
4. Attach new needle to pen device
5. Prime pen: dial 2 units, expel into air until drop visible at needle tip
6. Select dose as prescribed
7. Clean skin with alcohol wipe -- allow to dry 30 seconds
8. Pinch the skin at injection site (abdomen, lateral thigh, or upper arm)
9. Insert needle at 90 degrees (45 degrees if very thin patient)
10. Inject full dose -- hold for 10 seconds before withdrawing needle
11. Do NOT recap needle -- dispose immediately into sharps bin
12. Document dose, time, injection site, and BG reading in patient record

3. HYPOGLYCAEMIA MANAGEMENT

Definition: Blood glucose <4.0 mmol/L

Mild/Moderate (Patient Conscious and Able to Swallow):
  Step 1: Give 15-20g fast-acting carbohydrate:
          - 150-200 mL Lucozade Sport, OR
          - 3-4 glucose tablets, OR
          - 200 mL pure fruit juice
  Step 2: Wait 10-15 minutes, recheck BG
  Step 3: If BG still <4.0: repeat step 1
  Step 4: If BG >=4.0: give long-acting carbohydrate (e.g., plain biscuits, next meal)
  Step 5: Recheck BG 30 minutes later
  Step 6: Document and notify medical team

Severe (Patient Unconscious or Unable to Swallow):
  Step 1: Call for help immediately
  Step 2: IV Access: Give Glucose 10% 200 mL IV over 15 minutes
          OR if no IV access: Glucagon 1mg IM
  Step 3: Recheck BG 10 minutes after treatment
  Step 4: Once conscious and able to swallow: give long-acting carbohydrate
  Step 5: Call 2222 if patient does not regain consciousness within 10 minutes
  Step 6: Complete Datix incident report. Notify Diabetes team.

4. COMMON INSULIN TYPES AT NORTHGATE RH

Rapid-acting (mealtime) -- CLEAR:
  Novorapid (Insulin Aspart): Onset 10-20 min, Peak 1-2h, Duration 3-5h
  Humalog (Insulin Lispro):   Onset 10-20 min, Peak 1-2h, Duration 3-5h

Long-acting (background/basal) -- CLEAR:
  Lantus (Insulin Glargine):  Once daily, SAME TIME each day. Onset 1-4h. Duration 20-24h.
  Levemir (Insulin Detemir):  Once or twice daily. Duration up to 24h.

Mixed -- CLOUDY (must be mixed before use):
  NovoMix 30: Twice daily with meals. Onset 10-20 min. Dual peak.

WARNING: Long-acting insulins (Lantus, Levemir) are CLEAR but must NEVER be confused with rapid-acting clear insulins. ALWAYS read the label.

5. STORAGE
- Unopened insulin: Refrigerator 2-8 degrees Celsius (NOT in door of fridge)
- In-use insulin pen: Room temperature, protected from direct sunlight -- discard after 28 days
- Do NOT freeze insulin -- discard if frozen

BLOOD TRANSFUSION ADMINISTRATION SOP
Version: 2.4 | Effective: 15 February 2025 | Review: 15 February 2027
Department: Haematology / All Clinical Areas | Author: Dr. A. Patel, Consultant Haematologist

PURPOSE
To ensure the safe administration of blood and blood products to patients at Northgate Regional Hospital, in accordance with NICE Guideline NG24 and SHOT (Serious Hazards of Transfusion) recommendations.

IMPORTANT: Incorrect blood transfusion is a NEVER EVENT. The pre-transfusion checks described in this document are mandatory and must never be omitted.

1. PRE-TRANSFUSION: SAMPLE COLLECTION
- Label at the bedside, in the presence of the patient -- NEVER pre-label samples
- Label must include: full name, DOB, NHS number, date/time, collector's signature
- Group and Save (G&S) must be taken before any blood is ordered

2. PRESCRIBING BLOOD
- Blood must be prescribed on the drug chart using the Blood Transfusion section
- Each unit prescribed separately with: blood component, dose, rate, indication
- Only doctors and ANPs with Blood Transfusion competency may prescribe blood

3. BEDSIDE IDENTIFICATION CHECKS (MANDATORY -- 2-PERSON CHECK)

Both administering nurse AND a second checker must independently verify ALL of the following BEFORE connecting the blood:

CHECK 1 -- Patient Identity:
  Ask patient to state: Full name + Date of birth
  Check wristband: Name, DOB, NHS number
  Compare wristband against blood component label and compatibility label

CHECK 2 -- Blood Component:
  Blood group (ABO and RhD) matches patient
  Unit number on component matches compatibility label
  Expiry date and time -- do NOT use expired blood
  Appearance: look for leaks, abnormal colour, clots, haemolysis

CHECK 3 -- Documentation:
  Prescription matches component to be given
  Patient consent documented

WARNING: If there is ANY discrepancy -- STOP. Do not administer the blood. Contact the Blood Bank immediately.

4. ADMINISTRATION RATES
  Red cells:        Over 90-120 minutes per unit (max 4 hours from removal from fridge)
  Platelets:        Over 20-30 minutes
  FFP:              Over 30 minutes
  Cryoprecipitate:  Over 15-20 minutes

Observations required:
  - Baseline before transfusion starts
  - 15 minutes into first unit
  - Hourly during transfusion
  - On completion of each unit

5. TRANSFUSION REACTIONS

Mild Reaction (urticaria, pruritus only):
  - Slow transfusion rate
  - Give chlorphenamine 10mg IV
  - Continue with caution if symptoms resolve within 30 minutes

Moderate/Severe Reaction (fever, rigors, hypotension, back pain):
  - STOP transfusion immediately
  - Keep IV access open with 0.9% NaCl
  - Call medical team urgently
  - Do NOT discard the blood unit -- return to Blood Bank with reaction form
  - Complete a Datix incident report
  - Contact Blood Bank: ext. 4200

Anaphylaxis:
  - STOP transfusion
  - Call 2222 (Cardiac Arrest / Emergency)
  - Follow Anaphylaxis Protocol (MED-EMERG-001)
  - Give: Adrenaline 0.5mg IM, Chlorphenamine 10mg IV, Hydrocortisone 200mg IV

FALLS PREVENTION & MANAGEMENT SOP
Version: 3.1 | Effective: 01 April 2025 | Review: 01 April 2027
Department: Nursing / Patient Safety | Author: Sr. Angela Booth

PURPOSE
This SOP outlines the process for assessing, preventing, and managing patient falls across all inpatient wards at Northgate Regional Hospital.

1. FALLS RISK ASSESSMENT

All patients must be assessed using the Morse Falls Scale on:
- Admission
- Transfer to a new ward
- Any change in clinical condition
- Following a fall
- Every 72 hours as a minimum

Morse Falls Scale Risk Categories:
  Score 0-24  : Low risk   -> Standard precautions
  Score 25-44 : Moderate   -> Implement falls prevention protocol
  Score 45+   : High risk  -> Full falls prevention bundle + bed alarm

The score must be documented in the electronic patient record within 2 hours of admission.

2. FALLS PREVENTION BUNDLE (MODERATE-HIGH RISK)

Environment:
- Bed in lowest position at all times when patient is in bed
- Bed brakes applied
- Call bell within reach -- check every handover
- Clear path to bathroom
- Non-slip footwear provided and worn
- Adequate lighting, especially at night

Nursing Interventions:
- Hourly intentional rounding documented on the safety board
- Bed/chair alarm activated (do NOT disable without written clinical justification)
- Falls risk alert sticker on patient wristband and above bed
- Safety brief at every shift handover -- highlight high-risk patients

Medication Review:
- Flag patients on 4+ medications to prescriber for medication review
- High-risk medications: opioids, benzodiazepines, diuretics, antihypertensives, antipsychotics

3. POST-FALL MANAGEMENT

Immediate Actions (within 30 minutes):
1. Do NOT move the patient until assessed by a nurse
2. Call for medical assistance if injury suspected
3. Check vital signs: pulse, BP, oxygen saturation, GCS
4. Examine for signs of injury: head, neck, limbs, pelvis
5. If head injury suspected: perform neurological observations every 30 minutes for 4 hours
6. Comfort and reassure the patient

Documentation (within 2 hours of fall):
- Complete a Datix incident report (mandatory for ALL falls, including near-misses)
- Document in patient notes: time, location, circumstances, witnesses, injuries, actions taken
- Inform next-of-kin or nominated person
- Inform Consultant or on-call doctor

4. PATIENT & FAMILY EDUCATION
- Educate patient and family about falls risk and prevention strategies
- Advise patient to call for assistance before getting up
- Provide written information leaflet NUR-PI-FALLS-001

RELATED DOCUMENTS
- Morse Falls Scale Scoring Guide (NUR-SAFE-005-A)
- Post-Fall Neurological Observation Chart (NUR-OBS-003)
- Datix Incident Reporting Guide (QUAL-001)

OXYGEN THERAPY ADMINISTRATION SOP
Version: 2.0 | Effective: 01 October 2024 | Review: 01 October 2026
Department: Respiratory / Nursing | Author: Dr. Brian Lowe, Respiratory Consultant

PURPOSE
To ensure safe and effective administration of supplemental oxygen therapy to adult inpatients at Northgate Regional Hospital, in accordance with BTS Guideline on Emergency Oxygen Use in Adult Patients.

KEY PRINCIPLE: Oxygen is a DRUG. It must be prescribed (except in emergencies). Oxygen should be titrated to achieve target saturation, not given at fixed high flow rates to all patients.

1. OXYGEN PRESCRIBING

Oxygen must be prescribed on the drug chart with:
- Target oxygen saturation range
- Delivery device
- Flow rate range

Standard target saturations:
  Most patients:                         94-98% SpO2
  COPD or at risk of hypercapnic failure: 88-92% SpO2

WARNING: Giving high-flow oxygen to patients with COPD and chronic hypercapnia can suppress their hypoxic drive, worsen hypercapnia, and cause respiratory failure. Always check for COPD diagnosis and use 88-92% target range.

In emergencies (cardiac arrest, major trauma, anaphylaxis, severe asthma, CO poisoning):
Give high-flow oxygen via non-rebreathe mask 15L/min without waiting for prescription.

2. DELIVERY DEVICES AND FLOW RATES

Nasal Cannulae:
  Flow: 1-4 L/min
  Approximate FiO2: 24-36%
  Use: Mild hypoxaemia; comfortable for long-term use; patient can eat and speak

Simple Face Mask:
  Flow: 5-10 L/min (NEVER below 5 L/min -- risk of CO2 rebreathing)
  Approximate FiO2: 35-50%
  Use: Moderate hypoxaemia

Venturi Mask (Controlled Oxygen Therapy):
  Available: 24%, 28%, 35%, 40%, 60%
  Use: COPD patients requiring precise FiO2 control
  MUST use correct colour-coded Venturi valve at correct flow rate (printed on valve)

Non-Rebreathe Mask (NRM):
  Flow: 15 L/min minimum
  Approximate FiO2: up to 90%
  Use: Emergency situations only -- not for routine use

3. MONITORING

Monitor SpO2 continuously in acutely unwell patients.
For stable patients, document SpO2 and flow rate:
- Every 4 hours on a clinical observation chart
- 30 minutes after any change in oxygen therapy
- If any change in clinical condition

If SpO2 falls below target:
  - Increase flow rate by 1-2 L/min (or move to higher-delivery device)
  - Reassess within 5-10 minutes
  - If no improvement: call medical team urgently
  - If SpO2 below 90% unresponsive to oxygen: call 2222

If SpO2 above target (hyperoxaemia):
  - Reduce flow rate or wean to room air if clinically appropriate
  - Document in patient record

4. WEANING OXYGEN

Wean oxygen gradually when patient is clinically stable and SpO2 consistently within target range on current therapy. Do not abruptly discontinue oxygen in acutely unwell patients.

Step-down approach:
NRM 15L -> Simple mask 10L -> Simple mask 5L -> Nasal cannulae 4L -> Nasal cannulae 2L -> Air

5. HUMIDIFICATION

Humidification is required when:
- Flow rate >4 L/min via nasal cannulae for more than 12 hours
- Patient has tracheostomy
- Patient reports excessive nasal dryness or bleeding

Use: Bubble humidifier or heated humidification circuit
